Azure vs. AWS vs. GCP: Choosing the Right Cloud for Your Business

RP
Ryan Park Cloud & Network Engineer
· March 15, 2026 · 6 min read

Why Cloud Choice Matters

Choosing a cloud provider is one of the most consequential technology decisions a business will make. It affects everything from your monthly operating costs and compliance posture to how quickly your engineering team can ship new products. Yet the decision is often made by default — someone signs up for an AWS free tier, a few workloads migrate over, and suddenly you are locked in before anyone evaluated the alternatives.

At MTT Consulting, we see this play out regularly with mid-market companies that have between 50 and 500 employees. They are large enough to need real cloud infrastructure but not so large that they have a dedicated cloud architecture team weighing the options. The result is that cloud selection becomes an afterthought — and the costs compound over time.

This guide breaks down the three dominant platforms — Microsoft Azure, Amazon Web Services (AWS), and Google Cloud Platform (GCP) — through the lens of what actually matters to growing businesses: integration with your existing stack, cost predictability, compliance requirements, and long-term flexibility.

Microsoft Azure: Best for Microsoft-Heavy Environments

If your organization already runs on Microsoft 365, uses Active Directory for identity management, or relies on Windows Server workloads, Azure is the natural first choice. The integration is not just convenient — it is architectural. Azure Active Directory (now Entra ID) provides single sign-on across your entire Microsoft ecosystem, and licensing bundles through Enterprise Agreements can significantly reduce total cost of ownership.

Key Azure Strengths

  • Microsoft 365 integration: Seamless connectivity with Teams, SharePoint, Exchange Online, and Intune. Conditional access policies span both your SaaS and IaaS layers.
  • Hybrid cloud leadership: Azure Arc and Azure Stack HCI make it the strongest platform for organizations that need to keep some workloads on-premises while extending to the cloud.
  • Enterprise identity: Entra ID (formerly Azure AD) is the de facto standard for enterprise identity and access management, supporting SAML, OAuth, and SCIM out of the box.
  • Compliance certifications: Azure holds more compliance certifications than any other cloud provider, including FedRAMP High, CJIS, and ITAR — critical for government contractors and regulated industries.

"For businesses that are already invested in the Microsoft ecosystem, choosing a different cloud provider often means building integration bridges that Azure gives you for free."

Where Azure Falls Short

Azure's console experience and documentation have historically lagged behind AWS. The portal can feel overwhelming, and pricing calculators often produce estimates that diverge from actual bills. Azure's open-source and container ecosystem, while improving rapidly, still trails AWS and GCP in maturity for cloud-native development workflows.

Amazon Web Services: Best for Scale and Flexibility

AWS remains the market leader for a reason. With the broadest service catalog in the industry — over 200 fully featured services — AWS offers a solution for virtually every workload type. It is particularly strong for organizations that value infrastructure-as-code maturity, global availability zone coverage, and a massive ecosystem of third-party tooling and talent.

Key AWS Strengths

  • Breadth of services: From Lambda for serverless to SageMaker for machine learning to IoT Greengrass for edge computing, AWS has the deepest catalog across every category.
  • Startup-friendly: AWS Activate credits, a mature free tier, and the broadest community of developers make it the default choice for startups and fast-growing companies.
  • Global infrastructure: 33 regions and 105 availability zones (as of early 2026) make AWS the go-to for businesses that need low-latency global deployments.
  • Marketplace ecosystem: The AWS Marketplace has thousands of pre-built AMIs, containers, and SaaS integrations that accelerate deployment timelines.

Where AWS Falls Short

AWS pricing is notoriously complex. With dozens of billing dimensions per service — data transfer, API calls, storage classes, provisioned IOPS — it is easy for bills to surprise even experienced teams. Enterprise support plans are expensive, and the platform's sheer breadth can create decision fatigue for smaller teams trying to choose between three or four services that do similar things.

Google Cloud Platform: Best for Data and AI Workloads

GCP has carved out a distinct position as the platform of choice for data-intensive and AI-driven workloads. BigQuery remains the gold standard for serverless data warehousing, Kubernetes was born at Google, and their Vertex AI platform offers some of the most streamlined paths to deploying machine learning models in production.

Key GCP Strengths

  • Data analytics: BigQuery's serverless architecture, built-in ML functions, and separation of storage from compute make it the most cost-effective analytics platform at scale.
  • Kubernetes heritage: Google Kubernetes Engine (GKE) is the most mature and performant managed Kubernetes offering, with features like Autopilot mode that abstract away node management entirely.
  • Machine learning: Vertex AI, TensorFlow integration, and TPU access provide an end-to-end ML development lifecycle that is hard to match on other platforms.
  • Network performance: Google's private global fiber network delivers consistently low latency and high throughput, especially for data-heavy workloads.

Where GCP Falls Short

GCP has the smallest enterprise market share of the three, which translates to fewer third-party integrations, fewer certified professionals in the talent market, and occasional gaps in enterprise sales support. Google also has a reputation for deprecating products, which — fair or not — gives some enterprise buyers pause when making long-term infrastructure commitments.

Cost Comparison: How Pricing Models Differ

Direct cost comparisons between the three platforms are notoriously difficult because each uses different pricing structures, discount mechanisms, and billing granularity. That said, here are the patterns we consistently see when advising clients:

Factor Azure AWS GCP
Compute Pricing Competitive, especially with hybrid use benefits for Windows Server licenses Extensive instance types; Savings Plans and Reserved Instances offer up to 72% discount Sustained use discounts apply automatically; committed use discounts available
Data Egress First 100 GB/month free; standard rates after First 100 GB/month free; historically the most expensive at scale Most competitive egress pricing; premium tier and standard tier options
Storage Blob Storage tiers are cost-effective; integration with M365 adds value S3 is the gold standard with the most granular tiering (Glacier, Deep Archive) Unified storage model; Nearline/Coldline pricing is very competitive
Enterprise Discounts Enterprise Agreements; significant bundling with M365 and Dynamics Enterprise Discount Program (EDP); volume commitments Committed Use Discounts; less structured enterprise bundling
Free Tier 12 months free + always-free services 12 months free + always-free tier (broadest selection) $300 credit for 90 days + always-free tier

A key insight we share with every client: the cheapest cloud is the one you manage well. Without proper governance, tagging, and right-sizing, any platform will cost you more than it should.

Compliance and Security: Which Platform Suits Regulated Industries

For organizations in healthcare, financial services, government contracting, or legal services, compliance is not optional — it is a gating criterion. Here is how the platforms stack up:

Compliance Area Azure AWS GCP
HIPAA Supported with BAA Supported with BAA Supported with BAA
FedRAMP FedRAMP High (Azure Gov) FedRAMP High (GovCloud) FedRAMP High (Assured Workloads)
SOC 2 Type II Yes Yes Yes
PCI DSS Level 1 Level 1 Level 1
CJIS / ITAR Strongest support; Azure Gov is widely adopted Supported in GovCloud Limited availability
Data Residency Granular region controls; sovereign cloud options Region-level controls; Local Zones for edge Region-level controls; less sovereign options

Bottom line: All three platforms meet baseline compliance requirements for most industries. Azure and AWS pull ahead for government and defense workloads, while GCP is fully capable for HIPAA, SOC 2, and PCI DSS use cases. The real differentiator is how each platform implements guardrails — Azure Policy, AWS Config/Control Tower, and GCP Organization Policies — to enforce compliance at scale.

The Hybrid Reality: Why Most Businesses End Up Multi-Cloud

Here is a truth that vendor sales teams will not tell you: most mid-market and enterprise organizations end up running workloads across two or more cloud platforms. This is not always by design — it often happens through acquisitions, department-level decisions, or SaaS dependencies that pull in different cloud ecosystems.

The question is not whether you will use multiple clouds, but whether you will manage that complexity intentionally. A deliberate multi-cloud strategy involves:

  • Workload placement decisions: Running M365 and identity on Azure, compute-heavy workloads on AWS, and analytics on GCP — each playing to its strength.
  • Unified observability: Tools like Datadog, Grafana Cloud, or Azure Arc that provide a single pane of glass across environments.
  • Consistent security posture: Applying the same security policies, logging standards, and incident response procedures regardless of which platform hosts a given workload.
  • Cost governance: Centralized FinOps practices that track spending, enforce tagging, and optimize reserved capacity across all providers.

Multi-cloud done wrong is just multi-chaos. The goal is not to use every platform — it is to use the right platform for each workload while maintaining operational consistency.

MTT's Recommendation: How We Help Clients Choose and Migrate

At MTT Consulting, we do not have a preferred cloud vendor. We have a preferred outcome: our clients running on the infrastructure that best fits their business requirements, compliance obligations, budget, and in-house skill sets. Our approach follows a structured framework:

  1. Discovery and assessment: We inventory existing workloads, dependencies, licensing agreements, and compliance requirements. No recommendation is valid without understanding what you are starting with.
  2. Workload mapping: Each application and data set is evaluated against platform strengths. Not everything needs to move — some workloads are better left on-premises or in their current SaaS form.
  3. Cost modeling: We build detailed 3-year cost models that account for licensing, egress, support plans, and the operational cost of managing each platform. The cheapest compute price is meaningless if you need two additional engineers to manage the infrastructure.
  4. Migration execution: We handle the migration itself — from lift-and-shift for legacy systems to cloud-native refactoring for applications that benefit from platform-specific services.
  5. Ongoing optimization: Cloud costs and configurations drift over time. Our managed services include continuous right-sizing, security posture monitoring, and quarterly business reviews to ensure your cloud investment is delivering value.

Quick Decision Framework

If You... Consider
Run M365, Active Directory, and Windows Server Azure as your primary platform
Need the widest service selection and global reach AWS as your primary platform
Are building data pipelines, ML models, or analytics-first products GCP as your primary platform
Have strict government compliance requirements (CJIS, ITAR) Azure Government or AWS GovCloud
Want the simplest committed discount structure GCP with sustained and committed use discounts
Are already multi-cloud and need governance help Talk to MTT about a unified cloud strategy

Conclusion

There is no universally "best" cloud platform. Azure excels in Microsoft-integrated environments and hybrid scenarios. AWS leads in breadth of services and global scale. GCP is the frontrunner for data analytics and AI workloads. The right choice depends on your existing technology stack, compliance requirements, team expertise, and long-term business strategy.

What matters most is making that choice deliberately — with a clear understanding of the trade-offs — rather than drifting into a platform by default and discovering the limitations after you are locked in.
